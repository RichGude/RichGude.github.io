Commodities Background:

The following page discusses, visualizes, and interprets the current function, production, and valuation of five metal commodities: Aluminum, Copper, Iron, Nickel, and Zinc. A brief summary of each metal commodity under analysis, discussing its function and importance in various modern industries and principal uses, are detailed below:

Function and Purpose of Metal Commodities

Aluminum

With roughly one-third the density of steel and copper but sufficient toughness and strength for most commercial uses, ALUMINUM has become one of the most important and versatile metalic commodites in a multitude of industries. The low-eight, corrosion-resistant properties of aluminum is used in transportation industries for the frames of automobiles, aeroplanes, and marine vessels, construction for construction frames, doors, and siding, and consumer goods, including a multitude of appliances, utensils, tools, and packaging. By quantity, it is the second-most produced and consumed metal commodity after iron/steel.

Aluminum, in it's natural form, is often chemically bonded in bauxite formations (rock consisting of aluminum hydroxide minerals); for this reason, it is fairly resource-intensive to convert dried alumina into pure aluminum metal. Consequently, aluminum recycling forms a significant proportion of aluminum production; according to the United States Geologial Survey, in the year 2000, 36% of the US supply of aluminum metal came from recycled scrap.

COPPER is one of the oldest, industrially-used metal commodities on the planet, dating to its early incorporation into bronze armaments and tooling in the 2000s BCE. Known today for its exceptional malleability (i.e., propensity to be shaped and bent) and thermal and electrical conductivity, copper is highly utilized in electrical generation and distribution, telecommunication, and electronic product industries; these uses account for three-quarters of all copper consumption with building construction being the single largest market among its uses. By quantity, copper is the third-largest produced metal commodity after only iron/steel and aluminum, and similarly to aluminum, copper recycling makes up a significant portion of the ongoing copper supply.

Copper

Nickel

Similar to iron in its atomic structure and valence, NICKEL has many uses in alloying with iron in austenitic stainless steel applications; the vast majority of nickel consumption (upwards of 70%) is utilized in the production of these stainless steels and other speciality alloys. Large-constituent nickel alloys are known for their exceptional corrosion resistance, especially in high-temperature applications such as those in the aerospace and energy generation industries (namely for turbine blades, discs, and housing). Nickel is additionally used in catalyst, coinage, foundry products and plating, and, to a growing extent, rechargeable battery applications.

Though naturally abundant, IRON is not normally found in its metallic form often. For this reason, iron metal is processed from abundant iron ore deposits, generally consisting of iron oxide compounds. Metalic iron itself has few industrial resources, due to its low strength and corrosion resistance, so iron is alloyed with carbon, chromium, manganese, nickel, and other elements and processed in divergent manners to produce forms of steels with varying degrees of fracture toughness, tensile strength (the last two generally being inversely proportional to each other), corrosion resistance, and a host of other desirable traits.

Steel is far and away the most produced metal commodity on the planet: in 2015, over 1.5 billion metric tons of steel were produced world-wide; for comparison, aluminum, the second most-produced metal commodity, had less than 60 million metric tons produced during the same period (i.e., less than 4% of the produced tonnage of steel). There are few industries that do not utilize steel in some form.

Iron/Steel

Zinc

The principal industrial uses of ZINC are in its metallic form, accounting for three-quarters of all zinc consumption. In its metallic form, zinc is used to coat low-alloy steels (galvanized steel) as a low-cost alternative for corrosion-resistant stainless steel, as an alloy constituent in bronze, brass, and die-cast compounds, and in its rolled metallic form. The remaining quarter of zinc consumption is in rubber, chemical, and agricultural industries. By quantity, zinc is the fourth-largest produced metal commodity after iron/steel, aluminum, and copper.

Production Output of Metal Commodities

Data provided from the United States Geological Survey (USGS) records the production of various commodities (measured in metric tons) by country and year. The production output of Aluminum, Copper, Iron, Nickel, and Zinc are detailed in the infographic below.

Utilizing the infographic: The default view of the infographic is a heat map of the output quantity of Aluminum in metric tons per year per country. The default year is 2015, but the year value can be changed by dragging the cursor at the horizontal scroll bar on the bottom of the world map for any commodity, and the specific commodity under review can be adjusted with the labeled buttons above the year scroll bar.

Mousing over individual countries will highlight the country and display the specific comoddity production output of that country. The global output trend from 2011 to 2015 for any selected commodity is shown below the world map in the line graph by default. Clicking any specific country will show the country trend from 2011 to 2015 in the line graph, in lieu of the global trend. The line chart showing total production of commodities can be changed to a bar chart showing the change in production through each year from 2012 to 2015 by toggling the switch labeled with 'year change' in the line chart legend. Also within the legend, additional metal commodity trends can be compared to the current by selecting any or all from the remaining.

As can be seen in the above infographic and accompanying trend data, over the period observed (2011 to 2015), the production output of each commodity rose continuously for a few commodities in a few years: Aluminum and copper saw continuous production increase year over year, increasing 22.9% and 18.6%, respectively, over the period, with steel and zinc production following a similar trend until 2015 where each saw a decrease in production to 2012 levels (still increasing 5.2% and 2.4%, respectively, on the whole over the period). Nickel was the only commodity to see an overall drop in production from 2.34 million metric tons to 2.28, a comparatively small drop of 2.6% of total production).

For each metal commodity, individual countries dominate the production output: China dominates the aluminum, steel, and zinc markets especially for the former two, with roughly 10 times the production output of the second-largest producer of each (the Russian Federation for aluminum and the United States for steel); in this way, the aluminum and steel production from China represents roughly 50% or more of the global supply of either metal. For comparison, Chile, the largest single producer of copper, represents 30% of the global copper market, and the Philippines, the largest single producer of nickel, represents 25%.

Monthly Metal Commodity Prices

Data provided from the International Monetary Fund (IMF) records the monthly global market price of various commodities (measured in non-seasonally-adjusted, current US dollars($) per metric ton). The global market price of Aluminum, Copper, Iron, Nickel, and Zinc are detailed in the linear graph below.

Mousing over the infographic below will show individual monthly commodity prices, while mousing over any of the labels to the right of the infographic will highlight the individual metal commodity data on the infograph. It is important to recognize that the scale for the price axis (the left/Y axis) is logarithmic, so the price flunctuations between more expensive commodities, such as nickel, that seemingly match price fluctuations of cheaper commodities, such as iron, may only do so in relative perspective, not in absolute monetary values. Toggling the switch for 'linear' in the legend will change the axis from a log to linear axis, accordingly, but this scale hides the price changes of low-cost commodities (i.e., iron ore).

Global commodity price data distributed from the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis: Economic Research division (https://fred.stlouisfed.org/); data is copyright of the Internal Monetary Fund (IMF), displayed here as a non-commercial, primary research activity.

The logarithmic scale presented in the graphic belies the large fluctuations in commodity metal pricing in during the previous three decades: From a relatively low level with stable fluctuations in the 1990s, there is a sharp spike in commodity prices (excluding iron ore) in the mid-2000s, ending in ~2008 with the global finincial crisis and "Great Recession" of 2008, followed by a modest recovery in the early 2010s and tailing off in the mid-2010s. In the period from the commodity production data above (2011 to 2015), the price of each commodity trended down, despite, as previously noted, the production output trend of all but one commodity (nickel) trending up. These general trends agree with economic principle that as a supply (production) increases, prices tend to decrease as a set demand of each commidity drives competing suppliers to undercut each other.
